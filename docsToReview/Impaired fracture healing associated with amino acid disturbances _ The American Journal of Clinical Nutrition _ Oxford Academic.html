ABSTRACT

Background: Five percent to 10% of all fracture patients experience an inadequate healing process that results in a nonunion of fracture parts. Previous experimental studies have indicated the importance of sufficient nitric oxide production from arginine during normal fracture healing. However, during conditions of stress, such as inflammation, arginine availability can become limited, which may lead to a nonunion as a result of insufficient callus formation.

Objective: The aim of this study was to measure callus and plasma amino acid concentrations in patients with and without a fracture nonunion.

Design: Amino acid concentrations in plasma and callus were measured with HPLC in atrophic nonunions (n = 12) and compared with those in hypertrophic nonunions (n = 12), acute fractures (n = 15), and healed fractures (n = 8).

Results: Arginine (61 compared with 180 Î¼mol/mg; P < 0.0001), citrulline (13 compared with 44 Î¼mol/mg; P < 0.0001), and ornithine (25 compared with 149 Î¼mol/mg; P < 0.0001) in callus were significantly lower in atrophic-nonunion patients than in healed-fracture patients. In hypertrophic nonunions, arginine was significantly higher and ornithine was lower than in healed fractures. Plasma arginine concentrations were significantly lower in patients with hypertrophic nonunions (62 Î¼mol/L; P < 0.001) and acute-fracture patients (41 Î¼mol/L; P < 0.001) but not in atrophic-nonunion patients. Plasma ornithine concentrations were lower in all groups than in acute-fracture patients.

Conclusions: Amino acid concentrations were significantly changed in nonunion patients. Atrophic nonunions had lower concentrations of all amino acids, whereas hypertrophic nonunions had higher arginine and lower ornithine concentrations at fracture sites than did healed-fracture and acute-fracture patients.

INTRODUCTION

Five percent to 10% of all patients with a fracture experience deficient fracture healing that results in a nonunion of the fracture (1). This situation results from a multitude of conditions, including malnutrition, drug therapy, inadequate stabilization of fracture parts, and an inadequate blood supply (2, 3). A nonunion shows evidence of impaired healing on radiographic images as indicated by persistent fracture lines, sclerosis at fracture ends, a gap, and a hypertrophic or atrophic nonunion with an absence of callus formation (4). The impact of this problem for the patient is considerable because a nonunion results in increased morbidity, with patients having a decreased quality of life and associated psychosocial and functional impairments (5, 6). The economic impact is also high because a fracture nonunion requires multiple surgical interventions and frequently results in a compromised outcome (7, 8).

The maintenance of normal fracture healing requires accurate coordination and interaction between the environment and various components, such as signaling molecules, cells, growth factors, and extracellular matrix (2). An aspect suggested to be particularly important is sufficient production of nitric oxide (NO) from its sole precursor arginine at the fracture site. NO influences vascular reactivity (9) and stimulates bone cells (10) to regulate bone remodeling (11, 12).

Early upregulation of the NO synthase enzyme 2, which regulates the conversion of arginine into citrulline and NO, is a prerequisite for a normal inflammatory phase of fracture healing (10, 13, 14). Therefore, the production of NO and citrulline from arginine is of key importance for normal fracture healing (3).

In conditions of stress, such as wound healing and inflammation, arginine metabolism changes, and arginine availability is probably limited (15â18). These effects are partly due to the increased activity of arginase, which converts arginine into ornithine and urea and results in decreased plasma arginine concentrations. Furthermore, there is decreased endogenous conversion of citrulline into arginine in the kidney (13, 16, 17). Thus, arginine bioavailability may become a rate-limiting factor for NO production (a diagram of the arginine-NO pathway is shown in Figure 1). In experimental fracture healing, the improved availability of arginine and associated NO were shown to have beneficial effects on the fracture healing process (10). To our knowledge, the influence of these amino acid concentrations in nonunion development during fracture healing has not been evaluated in patients before.

We hypothesized that deficient concentrations of NO-related amino acids, especially arginine, at the fracture site might underlie atrophic-nonunion development, and therefore, we measured arginine, citrulline, and ornithine amino acid concentrations in callus and plasma in patients with and without a nonunion of fractures.

SUBJECTS AND METHODS

Patients

This study was approved by the medical ethics committee. Patients who attended the Department of Surgery, Maastricht University Medical Center, between November 2006 and November 2010, and had been diagnosed with a fresh fracture, a normally healed fracture of the lower extremity, or a nonunion, were included in the study after they provided written informed consent. Nonunion patients were identified on the basis of radiographic imaging â¥6 mo after the initial fracture. A nonunion was defined as a fracture that has ceased to show any evidence of healing as indicated by persistent fracture lines, sclerosis at fracture ends, a gap, and a hypertrophic or absent callus 6â8 mo after the occurrence of the fracture. Nonunion fractures were divided into atrophic and hypertrophic nonunions on the basis of a radiographic evaluation before surgery. This evaluation was done by a physician who was blinded to the results of the amino acid concentrations and a radiologist who specialized in trauma radiology (interobserver reliability: Îº = 0.86). Patients with a previous history of frequent bone fractures, infectious complications such as osteomyelitis, use of chronic corticosteroids or nitrovasodilating medication, or with severe metabolic disturbances, such as liver and renal insufficiency or diabetes mellitus, were excluded from the study because these conditions influence not only the normal bone healing process but also the amino acid concentrations. The control group consisted of patients with an acute fracture of the lower extremity and patients who were having metal implants removed after complete healing to relieve discomfort. The metal implants were removed 1 y after the initial fracture. Both control groups were matched with nonunion groups in terms of age, sex, and fracture localization.

Methods

Venous plasma samples were obtained from an antecubital vein preoperatively (before the induction of anesthesia) in the fasted state and collected in tubes containing EDTA. Plasma was prepared by immediate centrifugation at 2200 Ã g for 5 min at 4Â°C. A 100-Î¼L plasma aliquot was pipetted into a 1.5-mL Eppendorf tube that already contained 5.5 mg solid sulfosalicylic acid, vortex-mixed immediately, snap frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at â80Â°C until analysis.

Bone samples, which consisted of â¼50â100 mg wet tissue, were harvested from callus tissue of the nonunion site during the surgical procedure. In patients with an acute fracture, the same amount of bone sample was collected from the fracture site, whereas in patients who were having intramedullary osteosynthesis materials removed, 50â100 mg bone pulp was harvested from the intramedullary reaming. Callus samples or bone pulp samples were all harvested around old fracture parts at the margin of the bone. In healed-fracture patients, bone pulp was obtained through an additional intramedullary reaming process at the site of the old fracture parts. The amino acid concentrations were determined in the cambium layer of the periost-derived callus because this tissue contained pluripotent progenitor cells that were capable of endochondral bone formation after fracture healing (19). All samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at â80Â°C.

Before analysis, deproteinized plasma samples were thawed, vortex-mixed, and centrifuged for 10 min at 50,000 Ã g at 4Â°C in a Biofuge Stratos centrifuge (Heraeus). Next, 5 Î¼L of the clear supernatant fluid was diluted 100-fold with ice-cold water into a 1-mL WISP-style vial (Waters). Bone samples were weighed in 0.5 mL SARSTEDT screw-cap vials (SARSTEDT) that contained 0.1 g of 1-mm glass beads and 250 Î¼L ice-cold sulfosalicylic acid 5% (weight:volume). Samples were homogenized in a minibead beater (Biospect Products) for 30 s at maximum speed. Samples were centrifuged again at 50,000 Ã g at 4Â°C for 10 min, and 5 Î¼L clear supernatant fluid was diluted 100-fold in a 1-mL WISP-style vial (Waters). An amino acid analysis was performed by using HPLC after an automated precolumn derivatization by using o-phthaldialdehyde (OPA) as described previously (17). Briefly, at the start of each cycle, 5 Î¼L of the diluted sample, which was stored in the prechilled sample compartment of a WISP autosampler (Waters), was automatically mixed with 5 Î¼L OPA reagent. The resulting OPA-amino acid derivatives were separated on a 150 Ã 4.6-mm (inner diameter) Allsphere ODS 2 3 Î¼mol/L HPLC column (Grace) by using an acetonitrile gradient against an aqueous citric acid buffer (25 mmol/L; pH = 6.8) and detected by using fluorescence (330-nm excitation; 440-nm emission).

Statistics

Statistical analyses were performed with SPSS 15.0 software (SPSS Inc). All data are reported as means Â± SEMs. Amino acid concentrations in bone and callus are presented in micromoles per milligram of wet weight of the harvested tissue. Nonunion patients were divided into atrophic nonunions and hypertrophic nonunions and were compared with healed-fracture and acute-fracture patients. Studentâs t and chi-square tests were used to compare baseline characteristics and demographics between groups.

Amino acid concentrations were compared by using ANOVA with post hoc Bonferroni correction between patient groups. P < 0.05 was considered to be statistically significant. To our knowledge, before this study, there was no information available about arginine concentrations in bone. Therefore, these concentrations were estimated on the basis of healthy plasma concentrations (70 Â± 10 Î¼mol/L). In addition, we estimated that the arginine concentrations in bone would reduce by â¼30%. The estimated power to detect significant and clinically relevant differences between groups was a minimum of 6 patients per group and a minimum of 10 patients in the atrophic-nonunion patients group to reduce variance in this heterogeneous population.

Bone ornithine concentrations were significantly lower in atrophic-nonunion samples than in those in healed-fracture patients, acute-fracture patients, and hypertrophic-nonunion samples (all groups, P = 0.002) (Figure 4). The hypertrophic-nonunion ornithine callus concentrations were also lower than those in healed-fracture patients. The ornithine/arginine ratio, which is an indirect measure of arginase activity, was lower in both nonunion groups than in acute-fracture patients (Figure 5). The other amino acid concentrations in patients with an atrophic nonunion were similar to those in healed-fracture patients and acute-fracture patients. The majority of the amino acid concentrations in hypertrophic-nonunion patients were significantly higher than in atrophic-nonunion and healed- and acute-fracture patients (Table 2).

DISCUSSION

This study evaluated bone callus and plasma amino acid concentrations to investigate possible disturbances in the amino acid profile during impaired fracture healing. Patients with either an atrophic or hypertrophic nonunion had completely different arginine, citrulline, and ornithine concentrations in both blood and callus compared with healed- or acute-fracture patients. Overall, patients with an atrophic nonunion had lower concentrations of arginine, citrulline, and ornithine in the bone callus compared with in samples from healed-fracture patients. Hypertrophic-nonunion patients showed the opposite tendency, with higher concentrations of arginine and citrulline in the bone callus tissue. To our knowledge, this is the first human study to demonstrate a correlation between differences in local arginine concentrations in bone and the absence or presence of nonunion.

Several mechanisms may explain the link between the reduced arginine concentrations and abnormal bone healing in the atrophic-nonunion samples. Studies of normal fracture healing have shown an increased expression of NO synthesizing enzymes in chondrocytes and bone cells (20). A decrease in NO production stimulates osteoclast-induced bone resorption, which may result in decreased callus formation (20). The decreased arginine concentrations, as an NO precursor, in the callus of atrophic bone samples in our study are in line with these findings. In addition, neovascularization and vasoreactivity are essential during normal fracture healing, and NO also mediates this vasoreactivity and controls angiogenesis during fracture healing (9). Vascular reactivity in healing of rat femur fractures is stimulated by the NO stimulator acetylcholine (9)

Disturbances in amounts of arginine, which is the sole precursor of NO, might contribute to the development of impaired fracture healing because arginine is one of the conditionally essential amino acids involved in fracture healing (21). Comparable processes are present during wound healing or inflammatory conditions, which make sufficient arginine and NO availability essential for normal tissue regeneration (22, 23). The close relation and different time points of the upregulation of NO synthase and arginase regulates the healing process, NO to induce angiogenesis and to regulate the collagen synthesis (24), and ornithine produced by arginase as a precursor of collagen and polyamines (22, 24).

There are several conditions that can alter concentrations of arginine and related amino acids in plasma. Infectious conditions, diabetes mellitus, or previous fractures result in a decreased availability of arginine and its precursor citrulline. Inflammation involves increased catabolic and decreased anabolic pathways of arginine, which result in decreased plasma concentrations of arginine and citrulline (15â17). Other conditions that result in decreased arginine availability are renal or hepatosplanchnic dysfunctions because they compromise the availability and conversion of citrulline into arginine (24â27). Similarly, malnutrition contributes to impaired arginine availability and the associated impaired fracture healing (21). We excluded patients with these disorders from our study to minimize influences on amino acid concentrations.

The significant decrease in bone arginine concentrations in the atrophic-nonunion patients may be explained by increased use, or a possible inadequate supply, of arginine. In contrast, hypertrophic-nonunion patients showed a significant increase in callus arginine, citrulline, and ornithine concentrations. These results may have been due to an increased mobility of fracture parts caused by the inadequate stabilization and fixation of the fracture. If this increased mobility is limited to micromotion, fracture healing may still be sufficient but excess mobility of the fracture stops the calcification of the callus and results in hypertrophic nonunion (28). The significant metabolic response elicited in patients with a hypertrophic nonunion was interesting because the pathophysiology of this condition was previously regarded as purely biomechanical on the basis of poor fixation or diminished vascularity and not as metabolic.

Patients with an acute fracture and patients with an atrophic nonunion had similar bone arginine concentrations. The underlying cause may be the increased consumption of amino acids at the fracture site. Although this group, the atrophic non union patients, might also have a lower baseline concentration, our recent data did not support this because we measured baseline amino acid concentrations after the initial fracture and also after the atrophic nonunion development in the same patients and showed no decreased baseline concentrations in patients who eventually developed an atrophic nonunion (data not shown). Another possible explanation for the differences in concentrations may be the location of the fracture or nonunion. However, in this study, all patient groups exhibited a variety of fracture and nonunion locations with an equal distribution.

Trauma and the subsequent inflammatory response stimulate cell growth, differentiation, and cell signaling, which involve arginine being metabolized by arginase to ornithine and polyamines, such as proline. Polyamines are important mediators of cell growth, and proline is a precursor of collagen synthesis and the production of extracellular matrix molecules by fibroblasts (18, 29). These processes normally involve an increased demand and use of arginine, but these factors were not observed in our nonunion patients. The ornithine/arginine ratio (30, 31), which is an indirect measure of arginase activity, indicated a decreased arginase activity in the bone of nonunion patients. Especially in the atrophic nonunion group, this decreased arginase activity was accompanied by decreased arginine and ornithine availability, which indicated that there may have been impaired tissue regeneration. Another possible explanation of the decreased ornithine concentrations in this patient group may have been the enhanced demand for proline as a collagen precursor. Ornithine, proline, and glutamate are all part of a triangle relation. This bidirectional route may indicate a possible enhanced drain of ornithine to glutamate or proline on the basis of the increased demand for tissue regeneration. Indeed, we showed a trend toward enhanced glutamate concentrations in atrophic-nonunion patients, which may have been an indication for the increased demand for proline. However, proline is also an effective precursor of arginine through the conversion into ornithine and citrulline (24). We believe that the underlying relations between the several arginine catabolic pathways play an important role during fracture healing because there is a continuous demand for different compounds such as arginine, NO, and ornithine, polyamines, and proline. Therefore, this decreased ornithine/arginine ratio may not only be explained by a decreased arginase activity but possibly also by an enhanced demand for ornithine as proline precursor or by an enhanced demand for arginine and NO in these nonunions. More studies that harvest sufficient material for the determination of essential enzymes in this pathway will provide additional information.

Lower callus arginine concentration contrasted with normal plasma arginine concentrations of atrophic-nonunion patients. This result may indicate that a regional cause or distribution problem is responsible for the arginine deficiency rather than a systemic cause. The patients with an acute fracture had significantly lower plasma arginine concentrations and slightly lower citrulline concentrations that were comparable to concentrations previously reported by Long et al (32). Long et al (32) showed significantly decreased amino acid concentrations, especially as regards nonessential amino acids, because of an increased catabolic response (32).

Our study was subject to a few limitations. The study was conducted in a relatively small number of patients, and plasma and callus concentrations we shown to provide no indication of the activity of the enzymes in question. In view of the relatively small patient numbers, our study should be regarded as a pilot study for the design of a larger study that would use a dynamic in vivo evaluation of the arginine-NO metabolism in patients with and without a nonunion. Especially, early detection of an impaired fracture healing process may offer therapeutic options for local or systemic supplementation. Supplementation of the relevant amino acids arginine and citrulline may positively alter the eventual outcome of the healing process. Future clinical trials need to be conducted to investigate the role of this supplementation on fracture healing in acute or nonunion patients.

In conclusion, our results indicate that an atrophic-fracture nonunion may be related to an insufficient availability of arginine, citrulline, and ornithine. Our findings may have clinical implications and could result in an improved understanding of the nutritional requirements of high-risk trauma patients to prevent the development of nonunions.
